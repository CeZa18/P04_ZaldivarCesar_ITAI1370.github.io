QR Codes vs Barcodes

What are QR Codes and how do they work?

A QR code is a two-dimensional (2D) matrix barcode. Unlike traditional barcodes, it stores information both horizontally and vertically.

  • How it works: It uses a series of black squares arranged on a grid. Three large squares in the corners (position detection patterns) help the scanner determine the code’s orientation and structure.
  • Function: It typically triggers an action, such as opening a URL, downloading a file, or in the case of AR, launching a 3D digital overlay.

History of QR Codes

The QR code system originated in Japan and was developed by engineers at DENSO WAVE, an auto-parts manufacturing company for Toyota. The system was created in the 1990s to overcome the limitations of traditional barcodes, which could only store about 20 alphabetic characters.

By using both horizontal and vertical data storage, QR codes greatly increased information capacity. The addition of position detection patterns allowed scanners to read the codes quickly and accurately.

What are Barcodes and how do they work?

A barcode is a one-dimensional (1D) code represented by parallel black lines and white spaces.

Barcodes are read by laser or optical scanners that measure light reflected from the spaces between the bars.

They are commonly used for inventory tracking and retail purposes, such as UPC codes found on products.

QR Codes vs Barcodes

Feature Barcode (1D) QR Code (2D)
Data Capacity Low (20–25 characters) High (thousands of characters)
Dimensions Horizontal only Horizontal and vertical
Error Correction None High (up to 30% damage)
AR Utility Limited Excellent

Advantages and Disadvantages for AR

QR Codes for AR

Pros: High data capacity and easy recognition from different angles.

Cons: Can appear visually distracting in designs.

Barcodes for AR

Pros: Familiar and commonly used in retail environments.

Cons: Limited data and poor performance for 3D tracking.

Why are QR Codes more suitable for AR?

QR codes provide better support for augmented reality because of their square shape, high data capacity, and built-in error correction. These features allow QR codes to act as spatial anchors that keep digital objects aligned even as the user moves.
